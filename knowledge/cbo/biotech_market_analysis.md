# Biotech Market Analysis: A CBO's Framework

## Market Sizing: TAM, SAM, SOM
Every commercial assessment must quantify the addressable market:
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market)**: All patients with the disease globally × annual treatment cost. This is the theoretical maximum.
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)**: TAM filtered by geography (approved markets), diagnosis rate, treatment-eligible population, and healthcare access.
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)**: SAM × realistic market share given competitive landscape, positioning, and commercial capabilities. Typically 10-30% of SAM for a differentiated product.

**Benchmark thresholds for commercial viability:**
- Rare disease/orphan: SOM > $500M is attractive (high price, small population)
- Specialty (oncology, autoimmune): SOM > $1B typically required for standalone company
- Primary care (diabetes, cardiovascular): SOM > $3B needed due to lower pricing and high competition

## Competitive Landscape Analysis
Map all competitors across development stages:
- **Approved therapies**: Current standard of care. What are their limitations?
- **Phase 3**: High probability of approval. These define the competitive set at launch.
- **Phase 2**: Uncertain but could shift dynamics. Monitor for breakthrough data.
- **Phase 1/Preclinical**: Low probability but could leapfrog if mechanism is superior.

Key competitive questions:
- Is this first-in-class (new mechanism) or best-in-class (better version of validated mechanism)?
- What is the differentiation profile? (efficacy, safety, dosing convenience, route of administration)
- How many well-funded competitors are pursuing the same target? (>3 is a red flag for crowding)

## Intellectual Property Assessment
- **Composition of matter patents**: Strongest protection. Covers the molecule itself. ~20 years from filing.
- **Method of use patents**: Weaker, easier to design around. Covers specific indications.
- **Formulation/process patents**: Can extend lifecycle but rarely block generics/biosimilars.
- **Patent cliff risk**: When does exclusivity expire? Is there a lifecycle management strategy?
- **Freedom to operate**: Are there blocking patents from competitors? Is licensing required?

## Deal Structure and Partnerships
Common biotech deal structures:
- **Licensing**: Upfront payment ($10-100M) + milestones ($200-500M) + royalties (8-15% on net sales). De-risks development but gives up economics.
- **Co-development**: Shared costs and profits. Retains more economics but requires capital.
- **Acquisition**: Premium to market cap (30-100% typical). Exit strategy for small biotechs.
- **Royalty financing**: Non-dilutive capital by selling future royalty streams.

**Deal value benchmarks by stage:**
- Preclinical: $50-200M total deal value
- Phase 1: $200-500M total deal value
- Phase 2 (positive data): $500M-2B total deal value
- Phase 3 / approval: $1-10B+ (acquisition premiums)

## Pricing and Reimbursement
- **Orphan drugs**: $200K-500K/year. Justified by small populations and high unmet need.
- **Specialty biologics**: $50-150K/year. Requires demonstrated superiority over existing therapies.
- **Oncology**: $100-250K/year for novel mechanisms. Increasingly tied to outcomes-based contracts.
- **Primary care**: $5-20K/year. Volume-driven. Heavy payer negotiation and formulary battles.

Payer pushback is increasing globally. NICE (UK), IQWIG (Germany), and US PBMs increasingly demand real-world evidence of value. Drugs without clear differentiation face aggressive step-edit requirements and prior authorization barriers.

## Red Flags for Commercial Viability
- TAM < $500M with no orphan drug strategy
- More than 3 Phase 3 competitors targeting the same mechanism
- Patent cliff within 5 years of projected approval
- No clear differentiation from approved standard of care
- Payer resistance signals (ICER negative reviews, reference pricing trends)
- History of failed commercialization in the therapeutic area (e.g., Alzheimer's antibodies pre-2023)
