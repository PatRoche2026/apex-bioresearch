# Drug Discovery Pipeline: From Target to Market

## Target Identification and Validation
The drug discovery pipeline begins with target identification — finding a protein, gene, or pathway causally linked to disease. Validated targets have convergent evidence from human genetics (GWAS, Mendelian randomization), functional genomics (CRISPR screens, gene expression), and clinical observations. The strongest targets show genetic evidence in human populations, not just animal models.

Key validation approaches:
- **Genetic evidence**: GWAS hits, loss-of-function variants, Mendelian disease phenocopies
- **Functional genomics**: CRISPR knockout/knockdown, gene expression profiling, proteomics
- **Clinical correlation**: Biomarker studies, patient tissue analysis, failed drug repurposing data
- **Causal inference**: Mendelian randomization, colocalization analysis

## Hit Discovery and Lead Optimization
Once a target is validated, hit discovery identifies molecules that modulate it. Modality selection is critical: small molecules for intracellular targets with defined binding pockets, monoclonal antibodies for extracellular targets, RNA therapeutics for "undruggable" targets, gene therapies for genetic diseases. Lead optimization balances potency, selectivity, ADME properties, and safety.

## Preclinical Development
IND-enabling studies include GLP toxicology, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, and manufacturing process development. Species selection matters — many targets have different biology in rodents vs primates. CMC (Chemistry, Manufacturing, and Controls) for biologics is substantially more complex than for small molecules.

## Clinical Development Phases
- **Phase 1**: Safety, PK, dose-finding in healthy volunteers or patients (20-80 subjects)
- **Phase 2**: Proof-of-concept efficacy, dose-ranging (100-300 subjects). This is where most drugs fail.
- **Phase 3**: Confirmatory efficacy and safety in large population (1000-3000+ subjects)
- **Regulatory filing**: NDA/BLA submission, FDA/EMA review, advisory committee

## Key Success Factors
Overall probability of success from Phase 1 to approval is approximately 10-15%. Oncology has lower success rates (~5%) while rare diseases have higher rates (~25%). Programs with genetic evidence supporting the target have roughly 2x higher success rates. Biomarker-enriched trials show 3x higher Phase 2 success rates compared to unselected populations.

## Commercial Considerations
Market exclusivity depends on patent life, regulatory exclusivity (orphan drug, pediatric), and competitive landscape. First-in-class drugs command premium pricing but face adoption hurdles. Best-in-class drugs in established categories face pricing pressure but benefit from validated biology. Peak sales projections must account for patent cliffs, biosimilar entry, and payer dynamics.
